Nitrogen Reservoirs in Fulgurites

A Provenance Protocol and the Case Against Structural Si–N in Lightning Glass

Contract

This is the chemistry lane. It does not claim a coupled carbon–nitrogen–silicon system, a new mineral, a planetary circuit, or a mystical archive. It treats fulgurites as geochemical materials formed by lightning and asks a narrow, testable question:

«When nitrogen is detected in a fulgurite, what reservoir is it actually in?»

The answer matters because the sentence “there is nitrogen in the glass” can mean at least four different things: nitrogen bonded into the silica network, nitrogen trapped as molecular gas inside sealed vesicles, nitrogen adsorbed onto surfaces or weathering rinds, or nitrogen contained in organic residues. These are not the same claim. They do not require the same evidence. They do not carry the same meaning.

The operating rule is simple:

«Resolve the reservoir before naming the nitrogen.
No reservoir, no claim.»

This follows the project’s No-Crown rule: mythic labels may generate questions, but they do not support conclusions; the evidence strand cannot borrow authority from the narrative strand.

1. Abstract

Fulgurites are natural glasses formed when cloud-to-ground lightning fuses sand, soil, rock, or related substrates. Pasek, Block, and Pasek describe fulgurites as natural glasses produced by lightning and classify their morphologies across sand, clay, caliche, rock, and related forms.

This write-up argues that structural nitrogen, meaning nitrogen bonded directly into the silica network as Si–N or Si–O–N, should not be expected in ordinary terrestrial fulgurite glass. The reason is mechanistic: ordinary fulgurites form in air, in oxidizing conditions, and quench rapidly. Experimental work on nitrogen in silicate melts shows that nitrogen behavior is strongly controlled by oxygen fugacity; over broad conditions N₂ is the dominant dissolved species, while N³⁻-type structural species become significant only under reducing conditions.

The useful chemistry contribution is therefore not “we found structural nitrogen.” The stronger contribution is a provenance protocol: separate nitrogen into lattice, gas, surface, and organic reservoirs; test each with the right instrument; state an upper bound when the bonding test is mute; and avoid false positives from surface contamination, organic matter, SIMS recombination, or modern air infiltration.

The two most promising positive archive lanes are: first, sealed-vesicle gas, because Navarro-González and colleagues reported CO₂, CO, and NO in glassy bubbles from a Late Pleistocene Libyan Desert fulgurite; and second, carbon-correlated reduced phases, because lightning has been shown to reduce phosphate in fulgurites and iron silicides have been reported and reviewed in fulgurite contexts.

The expected output is therefore:

1. an upper bound or negative result for structural Si–N;
2. provenance-resolved accounting of any nitrogen detected;
3. replication/extension of sealed-vesicle gas archive signals;
4. spatial testing of carbon-correlated reduced phosphorus and iron-silicide phases;
5. a strictly controlled pilot on post-strike bioreceptivity, kept separate from clean nitrogen chemistry.

2. Background: What Fulgurites Are

A fulgurite is a natural melt product produced when lightning delivers enough energy into a substrate to fuse, vitrify, and sometimes vaporize mineral grains. In silica-rich settings, the resulting material is commonly dominated by lechatelierite, amorphous silica glass. The morphology and chemistry of a fulgurite depend on the struck substrate, the discharge conditions, moisture, organic content, metal contamination, and cooling history.

This matters because fulgurites are not uniform laboratory glasses. They are small, violent, heterogeneous melt products. A single specimen can contain clean silica glass, mixed groundmass, bubbles, charred residues, metallic inclusions, weathered exterior surfaces, and cracks that later admit fluids or contaminants. That heterogeneity is exactly why nitrogen provenance must be resolved before interpretation.

The chemistry question is not simply:

«Is there nitrogen?»

The better question is:

«Where is the nitrogen, what chemical form is it in, and how do we know it belongs to the lightning event rather than to later contamination or biological/weathering overprint?»

3. The Actual Gap

Fulgurites can preserve event information. That is not the speculative part.

The sealed-vesicle gas result is already real enough to treat as a target for replication: Navarro-González and colleagues reported CO₂, CO, and NO trapped inside glassy bubbles in a Libyan Desert fulgurite and used those gases, plus isotopic and C/N information, to infer paleoecological context.

Lightning-driven redox chemistry is also a real lane. Pasek and Block reported that cloud-to-ground lightning can reduce phosphate in fulgurites, with reduced phosphorus found in several specimens.  Iron silicides have also been reviewed in fulgurite occurrences, with the key issue being the extremely reducing microenvironments required for their formation.

The gap is more specific:

«Positive nitrogen signals in fulgurites are easy to overinterpret unless they are assigned to the correct reservoir.»

A nitrogen peak, by itself, is not a structural claim. A surface nitrate signal is not event nitrogen. A CN signal is not automatically lattice nitrogen. A SiN⁻ secondary ion in SIMS is not automatically proof of a pre-existing Si–N bond. A bubble gas signal is not the same as a bonded network signal.

The chemistry lane is therefore a sorting machine.

4. Why Structural Si–N Is Not Expected

Structural Si–N in ordinary fulgurite glass is a predicted negative.

That is the central chemistry claim.

The argument has four parts.

4.1 Nitrogen speciation in silicate melts is redox-controlled

Nitrogen incorporation in silicate melts depends strongly on oxygen fugacity. Under relatively oxidizing conditions, nitrogen is generally present as molecular N₂ rather than as network-bonded nitride. Under highly reducing conditions, nitride-like N³⁻ species can become more significant and enter melt structures.

That means ordinary air-formed lightning glass is not the natural setting where one should expect structural nitride or silicon oxynitride formation.

4.2 Ordinary fulgurites form in air

Most terrestrial fulgurites form when lightning strikes sand, soil, rock, or sediment in the atmosphere. That setting is not a deliberately reducing furnace, not an ammonia treatment, and not a nitride-starting-material synthesis environment.

Even if tiny reduced microzones form locally, the broad prior remains: structural Si–N in clean silica glass should be absent or below meaningful detection.

4.3 Silicon oxynitride requires special conditions

Natural silicon oxynitride, known as sinoite, was identified as a meteoritic mineral in an enstatite chondrite, a highly reduced extraterrestrial context, not an ordinary terrestrial lightning-glass product.  Confirmed terrestrial nitride minerals are exceptionally rare; siderazot, an iron nitride from a fumarolic setting, was historically reported once and only much later confirmed and structurally characterized.

The base-rate argument is important: nature can make nitrides, but ordinary struck soil is not the expected place to make structural silicon nitride or silicon oxynitride glass.

4.4 The honest expected result is an upper bound

If EELS does not see nitrogen in fresh interior glass, that does not prove “nothing interesting happened.” It means the structural-bonding claim is unsupported and should be reported as an upper bound. The chemistry document already states this as the honest default: structural Si–N is expected absent, and the value of the result is strongest when paired with provenance-resolved accounting of nitrogen in other reservoirs.

So the clean claim is:

«Structural Si–N in ordinary fulgurite glass is not expected. If nitrogen exists, the first job is to determine whether it is gas, surface, organic, or truly lattice-bound.»

5. The Four Nitrogen Reservoirs

Any nitrogen signal must be sorted into one of four reservoirs.

R1 — Lattice / Structural Nitrogen

Definition: nitrogen bonded into the silica glass network as Si–N or Si–O–N.

Prior expectation: absent or below detection in ordinary fulgurite glass.

What would confirm it: spatially coherent nitrogen in fresh interior lechatelierite, above detection, with N-K edge ELNES consistent with Si–N bonding, distinguished from nitrate, organic nitrogen, trapped gas, beam contamination, groundmass mixing, and SIMS recombination.

Status: predicted negative.

R2 — Occluded Molecular Gas

Definition: nitrogen-bearing gas trapped inside sealed vesicles, potentially including NO, NOₓ, N₂ mixtures, CO, and CO₂.

Prior expectation: plausible and important.

What would confirm it: sealed-vesicle crush or stepped-heating release that differs from modern air, open-vesicle gas, rind contamination, and procedural blanks. Gas ratios, δ¹³C, δ¹⁵N, and where possible ¹⁴C context would support provenance, but isotopes alone should not be overclaimed.

Status: plausible positive archive lane.

R3 — Surface / Weathering Nitrogen

Definition: nitrate, ammonium, adsorbed nitrogen, handling contamination, storage contamination, soil/weathering input, or nitrogen on cracks and rinds.

Prior expectation: common.

What would confirm it: exterior-to-interior gradients, concentration on cracks/rinds, disappearance in fresh interior glass, association with open surfaces, and sensitivity to cleaning/fracturing protocols.

Status: default contamination lane unless proven otherwise.

R4 — Organic Residue Nitrogen

Definition: nitrogen in charred biomass, trapped carbonaceous particles, PAHs, microbial films, bioalteration products, or soil organic matter.

Prior expectation: mixed; possible, but must be separated from lattice and gas claims.

What would confirm it: co-location with carbon phases, CN-rich signals, organic ELNES/Raman signatures, textural association with residues rather than clean lechatelierite, and separation from biological overprint.

Status: plausible but dangerous if confused with R1.

Physical entrapment is not bonding. Organic nitrogen is not lattice nitrogen. Surface nitrogen is not event nitrogen without controls. That is the whole blade.

6. Analytical Decision Tree

6.1 Sample triage

Start by separating physical domains before analysis:

1. fresh interior lechatelierite;
2. mixed groundmass;
3. sealed vesicles;
4. open vesicles;
5. weathered rind;
6. cracks and pores;
7. carbon-rich residues;
8. biologically colonized or altered zones.

Do not analyze all of these as if they are one material. A bulk result is almost guaranteed to smear reservoirs together.

6.2 NanoSIMS

NanoSIMS is useful for sensitive nitrogen detection and spatial mapping. It can map CN⁻, SiN⁻, NO⁻, and related secondary ions across interior glass, residues, cracks, and rinds.

But NanoSIMS cannot, by itself, prove a pre-existing Si–N bond. Secondary ions can form during sputtering, and CN⁻ can reflect organic nitrogen, adsorbed nitrogen, or analytical recombination. The correct role of NanoSIMS is localization and reservoir targeting, not final bonding assignment.

6.3 EELS N-K ELNES

EELS is the decisive bonding-environment lane if nitrogen is present above detection. N-K edge fine structure can, in principle, distinguish bonding environments such as nitride-like, nitrate-like, and organic nitrogen.

But EELS has an honest limit: if structural nitrogen is absent or below detection, it cannot fingerprint what is not there. In the predicted default case, the result is not a dramatic positive; it is an upper bound on R1.

6.4 Vacuum crush, stepped heating, GC-MS, isotopes

This is the strongest lane for R2, the sealed-vesicle gas archive.

The ideal workflow is paired sealed-vs-open vesicle extraction, procedural blanks, modern-air references, stepped-heating release, GC-MS gas composition, and δ¹³C/δ¹⁵N measurement. If ancient preservation is claimed, ¹⁴C context becomes important.

A gas archive claim strengthens only if sealed vesicles release gas that is compositionally or isotopically distinct from modern air, soil infiltration, open vesicles, and weathered surfaces.

6.5 XANES / EXAFS for phosphorus and iron

XANES and EXAFS are appropriate for the redox archive lane, especially for phosphorus and iron oxidation states. This lane should test whether reduced P or Fe phases correlate spatially with carbon-rich zones, rather than merely identifying isolated reduced phases.

This matters because carbon can locally drive reduction in lightning melts. But that does not automatically imply nitrogen is reduced into the silica lattice. The chemistry document explicitly keeps those mechanisms separate.

6.6 Raman, XRD, SEM-EDS, microprobe

These are context tools:

- Raman: carbon phases, glass structure, possible organic/residue signatures;
- XRD: crystalline reduced phases;
- SEM-EDS: texture, inclusions, phase context;
- electron microprobe: transects across carbon, phosphorus, iron, silicon, and substrate heterogeneity.

They are powerful for mapping and selecting targets, but they do not replace direct nitrogen bonding tests.

6.7 XPS, FTIR, and surface-only methods

XPS is surface-biased and therefore dangerous unless used on freshly fractured interiors with strict controls and depth profiling. FTIR and Raman assignments for Si–N in complex glass are not decisive because vibrational features can overlap or be ambiguous.

Any surface-only nitrogen detection should be treated as R3 until proven otherwise.

7. Sub-Study A: Sealed-Vesicle Gas Archive

The strongest positive nitrogen lane is not structural Si–N. It is sealed-vesicle gas.

Known starting point: Navarro-González and colleagues reported CO₂, CO, and NO in glass bubbles from a Late Pleistocene Libyan Desert fulgurite, using those gases to reconstruct paleoecological context.

Research question

Can sealed vesicles in multiple fulgurites preserve event-linked gas signatures distinguishable from modern air, soil infiltration, and surface contamination?

Novelty

The novelty is not “gas in one fulgurite exists.” The novelty would be:

1. multi-specimen reproducibility;
2. paired sealed-vs-open vesicle comparison;
3. stepped-heating release structure;
4. explicit modern-air and procedural blanks;
5. provenance-resolved nitrogen accounting.

Methods

- micro-CT or microscopy to identify sealed vesicles;
- vacuum crush of sealed vesicle domains;
- stepped heating;
- GC-MS for CO₂, CO, NO, N₂, O₂, Ar, and related species;
- δ¹³C and δ¹⁵N where sample size permits;
- ¹⁴C context where ancient preservation is claimed;
- comparison against open vesicles and weathered rinds.

Falsifiers

The gas archive claim weakens or fails if:

- sealed-vesicle gas matches modern air or soil gas;
- N₂/O₂/Ar ratios indicate infiltration;
- open and sealed vesicles are indistinguishable;
- stepped heating does not separate occluded gas from adsorbed contamination;
- carbon dating returns modern carbon where ancient preservation is claimed.

Caveat

δ¹⁵N should not be overclaimed. Lightning-fixed nitrogen and atmospheric/soil nitrogen can overlap isotopically. Isotopes support provenance; they do not single-handedly prove it.

8. Sub-Study B: Carbon-Correlated Reduced Phases

The second positive lane is redox chemistry involving phosphorus and iron.

Pasek and Block showed that cloud-to-ground lightning can reduce phosphate in fulgurites, and Hess and colleagues later argued that lightning-produced reduced phosphorus could be significant for prebiotic chemistry under early Earth conditions.  Iron silicides are also reported in fulgurites and reviewed as products associated with highly reducing conditions.

Research question

Are reduced phosphorus and iron-silicide phases spatially correlated with carbon-rich zones in fulgurites?

Why carbon matters

Carbon-rich substrate or organic matter can create local reducing microenvironments during a lightning melt. That may help explain reduced phosphorus species or iron-silicide formation.

But this is a bounded claim. Carbon-driven reduction in P and Fe systems does not mean carbon reduces nitrogen into the silica lattice. The nitrogen lattice claim remains separate and predicted negative.

Methods

- SEM-EDS phase mapping;
- Raman mapping of carbon phases;
- XRD for crystalline reduced phases;
- P and Fe XANES/EXAFS for oxidation state;
- microprobe transects across carbon-rich and carbon-poor zones;
- comparison with substrate/protolith controls;
- audit for conductor contamination, metallic debris, and detrital reduced phases.

Falsifiers

The redox archive claim weakens or fails if:

- reduced phases do not correlate with carbon-rich zones;
- phases trace to wire, fence, conductor, or anthropogenic contamination;
- phases are detrital rather than lightning-produced;
- reduced products appear equally in non-strike substrate controls.

9. Pilot: Post-Strike Bioreceptivity

Biological colonization of silicate glass is not a new claim. The narrow question is whether lightning-altered glass has a colonization or alteration signature distinguishable from ordinary glass, substrate disturbance, moisture, nutrients, surface area, and pH.

This must remain a pilot, not a co-equal central hypothesis.

Research question

Does strike-altered glass chemistry produce a biological colonization or alteration response beyond ordinary glass/substrate controls?

Required controls

- experimental fulgurite generated under realistic discharge conditions;
- sterilized fulgurite glass;
- kiln or thermal glass;
- protolith from the same site;
- disturbed non-strike soil;
- nearby matched non-strike substrate;
- time series;
- multi-site replication;
- blinded scoring.

Experimental fulgurite generation has been reported under controlled discharge conditions, which makes it a useful control route for this pilot.

Boundary

Biologically colonized specimens must not be used for clean structural-nitrogen measurements. Biology and clean nitrogen chemistry need separate sample streams.

10. Cosmochemical Context: Why These Elements Keep Showing Up

This part is context, not proof of any fulgurite-specific claim.

The recurring elements in the broader walk — carbon, nitrogen, oxygen, silicon, sulfur, phosphorus, and iron — are not random symbolic furniture. They sit inside real cosmochemical and geochemical supply chains.

Carbon, nitrogen, and oxygen are central to stellar nucleosynthesis and later planetary chemistry. The CNO cycle is a real hydrogen-burning process in stars, and nitrogen has a distinct stellar-processing lane rather than simply being “the next symbolic element.”  Massive-star evolution also produces onion-shell structures involving carbon, oxygen, silicon, sulfur, and iron-group endpoints; silicon burning is the final fusion stage leading toward iron-core collapse.

That cosmic context helps explain why these elements recur in bodies, rocks, atmospheres, soils, and glasses. But it does not prove a coupled C–N–Si fulgurite mechanism.

The clean phrasing is:

«Nucleosynthesis explains why the materials are available.
Local geochemistry explains what lightning does to a sample.
The two may touch as context, but they do not fuse into evidence.»

11. Upgrade Criteria
Upgrade R1, structural nitrogen, only if all of this appears:
fresh interior lechatelierite, not rind or mixed groundmass;
nitrogen above detection;
spatially coherent N signal not explained by cracks, surfaces, organics, or contamination;
N-K ELNES consistent with Si–N bonding;
controls excluding nitrate, organic nitrogen, trapped gas, beam buildup, mounting media, adhesives, and SIMS recombination.
Without those, the correct R1 output is an upper bound.
Strengthen R2, sealed gas, if:
sealed vesicles differ from open vesicles;
gas ratios differ from modern air and soil gas;
stepped heating separates occluded gas from adsorbed contamination;
isotopes support non-modern or event-linked provenance;
results replicate across specimens.
Strengthen the redox archive if:
reduced P or Fe phases correlate with carbon-rich zones;
phases are absent from comparable non-strike substrate;
conductor contamination is excluded;
P/Fe oxidation-state maps support lightning-driven reduction;
results replicate across samples.
Strengthen the bioreceptivity pilot if:
colonization differs from thermal glass and non-strike substrate;
the difference tracks strike-altered mineral/glass chemistry;
the result survives sterile, disturbed-soil, protolith, and experimental-fulgurite controls;
scoring is blinded and replicated.
12. Mandatory Controls
Analytical controls
procedural blanks for extraction, mounting, polishing, and storage;
nitrogen-free fused silica through identical preparation;
protolith from the same site;
sealed-vs-open vesicle pairs;
fresh interior vs rind gradient;
clean lechatelierite vs mixed groundmass;
crack and pore mapping;
curation audit for adhesives, consolidants, acid treatment, storage nitrate, animal/bird contamination, and handling residue;
beam-induced C/N buildup checks;
separate sample streams for biology and clean nitrogen chemistry.
Interpretation controls
never treat surface nitrogen as event nitrogen without gradient and blank controls;
never treat SiN⁻ SIMS signal alone as Si–N bonding;
never treat organic CN signal as lattice nitrogen;
never treat sealed gas as structural nitrogen;
never treat redox P/Fe behavior as proof of reduced nitrogen.
Safety controls
No DIY lightning. No high-voltage arc rigs outside institutional laboratories. No fulgurite collection during active storms. No sealed pressure vessels. No unknown-gas extraction outside proper lab infrastructure. No heating mystery materials. No chemical synthesis attempts. All serious testing belongs in institutional labs with trained personnel, approved procedures, ventilation, and appropriate instrumentation.
13. Expected Results
The likely result is not a dramatic discovery of storm-made silicon nitride glass.
The likely result is:
no detectable structural Si–N in fresh interior lechatelierite;
a defensible upper bound on R1;
nitrogen detected mainly as surface/weathering, organic residue, or sealed-vesicle gas;
possible replication of occluded CO₂/CO/NO archive signals in well-preserved vesicles;
possible carbon-correlated reduced P and Fe phases in specific local microenvironments;
no general coupled carbon–nitrogen–silicon mechanism.
That is not a failure. That is the chemistry getting sharper.
The strongest honest contribution is:
A reservoir-deconvolution protocol for fulgurite nitrogen, a mechanistic predicted-negative for structural Si–N, and a controlled replication/extension program for sealed-vesicle gas and carbon-correlated redox archives.

Final chemistry statement
Fulgurites can preserve chemical information, but not every preserved signal is the same kind of signal. In ordinary fulgurite glass, structural Si–N is not the expected result. The real work is to separate lattice, gas, surface, and organic nitrogen; quantify what each reservoir can and cannot prove; and let the instruments decide where the story stops.
The mythic phrase “glass remembers” is allowed to point. The chemistry lane asks: as what — gas, bond, surface, or residue?
